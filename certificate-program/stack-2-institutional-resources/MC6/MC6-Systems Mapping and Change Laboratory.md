## Cross-National Activity System Mapping and Governance Under Intervention

**Format:** Teams of 3–4  
**Primary Frameworks:** Engeström Activity System, Contradiction Typology, Change Laboratory  
**Secondary Frameworks:** IDEO (Viability / Feasibility / Desirability), Strategyzer (Structural Comparison Only)  
**Estimated Duration:** 6–8 weeks

## Course Description

After visualizing institutional implementation in MC5, MC6 asks a harder question: what would break if the proposed audiovisual installation were formally introduced into each museum’s operational system?

This microcredential moves from artifact placement to systemic consequence. Students treat their MC5 installation and educational trivia corpus not as finished designs, but as interventions inserted into real institutional ecosystems governed by funding structures, curatorial mandates, national memory cultures, and public trust expectations.

Students do not design new artifacts in this microcredential.

They interrogate the systemic consequences of introducing their MC5 installation proposal and educational trivia corpus into:

1. The Canadian War Museum (Ottawa)
2. The National WWII Museum (New Orleans)

The guiding question:

**If this intervention were introduced, what systemic tensions would surface?**

The intervention under examination is not abstract. It includes:

- A museum-based audiovisual installation
- A walk-through visitor engagement system
- A staged backdrop for a cross-national WWII trivia championship (modeled on _Reach for the Top_)
- A curated knowledge corpus contributed by students

The question is not whether the design is creative — it is whether it is systemically sustainable.

## **Theoretical Grounding**

Change Laboratory builds directly on Vygotsky’s mediated action model (S → X → R), in which:

- **S (Stimulus)** represents the problematic situation.
- **X (Mediating Artifact)** enables new forms of response.
- **R (Response)** becomes reconfigured action.

In institutional systems, tensions or contradictions act as the **first stimulus** — they expose instability.

A **second stimulus** is introduced not as a solution, but as a tool that allows actors to reorganize activity. In MC6, students simulate this intervention process.

---
## **The First Stimuli: Material and Temporal Disruption**

In this microcredential, two primary stimuli are introduced into existing institutional activity systems:
###  Spatial Intervention Stimulus**

The introduction of a new audiovisual installation into the museum’s physical architecture. This alters:

- Walkthrough rhythm
- Visual hierarchy
- Visitor attention flows
- Curatorial emphasis
- Technological infrastructure load
- Interpretive framing environment

This is a **material disruption** to established institutional mediation.

### **Temporal Event Intervention Stimulus**

The proposed staging of a cross-national WWII trivia championship final within museum space. This alters:

- Visitor expectations (from contemplative to competitive)
- Time structure (from self-paced browsing to scheduled gathering)
- Social configuration (individual navigation → collective spectacle)
- Acoustic dynamics
- Staff allocation
- Institutional branding posture

This is a **temporal and social disruption** layered onto spatial mediation.

---

These two stimuli are not hypothetical abstractions. They are:

- Concrete insertions into operational institutional systems.
- Designed to surface structural tensions already latent in the system.

The guiding question becomes:

  >What systemic contradictions are activated when spatial and temporal interventions are introduced into established museum activity systems?

---
## **The Second Stimulus: Previsualization as Mediating Artifact**

In Vygotsky’s S → X → R model:

- The first stimulus exposes tension.
- The second stimulus enables reorganization.
  
In MC6, the second stimulus is:

> The previsualization system developed in MC5.

The previsualizations function as:

- Imaginative rehearsal space
- Cognitive simulation device
- Structural diagnostic lens
- Institutional foresight tool
  
They allow stakeholders to:

- See potential conflicts before implementation
- Visualize visitor flow disruptions
- Detect narrative dominance shifts
- Observe spectacle escalation risks
- Identify acoustic or spatial strain points
- Anticipate governance tensions
  
The previsualization is not a marketing artifact.

It is a **mediating tool that enables institutional actors to reframe their activity before committing resources.**

---

## **Why This Matters in Vygotsky’s Terms**

The museum system initially responds to new installation proposals reactively. The previsualization transforms reaction into mediated deliberation.

Instead of:

Stimulus → Immediate Resistance or Acceptance

We get:

Stimulus → Mediating Visualization → Reflective Reconfiguration

The second stimulus allows:

- Structural rethinking of object
- Redistribution of division of labour
- Reinterpretation of rules of engagement
- Recalibration of technological mediation

The intervention becomes thoughtful rather than disruptive.

## Part II — Contradiction Identification

Students must move beyond identification. For each contradiction, teams must articulate:

- What institutional risk it generates
- Who absorbs that risk
- Whether it threatens legitimacy, funding, or public trust
- Whether it can be reconfigured without distorting archival gravity

---
## **Primary Contradictions**

_Contradictions internal to a single node of the activity system._

These are tensions that exist within an institutional element before any intervention is introduced. Students must identify and articulate contradictions such as:

- **Mission vs. Engagement Intensity**
    A museum’s mandate to preserve historical gravitas may conflict with pressure to increase immersive or emotionally charged visitor experiences.
    
- **Educational Mandate vs. Brand Visibility**
    Institutions may aim to serve public education while also cultivating donor recognition and institutional brand amplification.
    
- **Public Access vs. Curatorial Control**
    The desire for broad public accessibility may conflict with strict archival gatekeeping and interpretive authority.
    
- **Technological Innovation vs. Preservation Ethos**
    Pressure to modernize through immersive technology may conflict with preservation-centered curatorial values.
    
- **Revenue Stability vs. Scholarly Neutrality**
    Funding dependencies (public or private) may subtly influence interpretive framing priorities.

For each identified primary contradiction, students must specify:

- Where it resides in the system
- Whether it is structural or episodic
- What risks it generates if intensified

---
## **Secondary Contradictions**

_Contradictions between two nodes within the same activity system._

These emerge when institutional elements interact. Students must analyze tensions such as:

- **Donor Expectations (Exchange Logic) vs. Archival Fidelity (Rules / Mission)**
    Sponsorship visibility requirements may conflict with restrained curatorial presentation.
- **Visitor Engagement Goals (Consumption Logic) vs. Division of Labour**
    Increased interactivity may require staffing, technical support, or training that exceeds institutional capacity.
- **Technological Tools vs. Community Norms**
    Installation of large-scale audiovisual screens may challenge established museum walk-through rhythms or visitor expectations.
- **Educational Trivia Competition vs. Curatorial Gravity**
    Competitive formats may risk trivializing solemn historical material.
- **Institutional Narrative Framing vs. Archival Source Integrity**
    Interpretive overlays may create tension between storytelling clarity and documentary nuance.

For each secondary contradiction, students must:

- Identify the interacting nodes
- Explain how the tension manifests operationally
- Evaluate whether it can be resolved, mitigated, or requires systemic redesign

---
## **Cross-System Contradictions**

_Contradictions emerging from comparison between the Canadian and U.S. institutional models._

These are not internal tensions, but divergences between national activity systems. Students must examine contrasts such as:

- **Public Funding Model (Canada) vs. Philanthropic Dependence (U.S.)**
    How funding structures alter donor visibility expectations and interpretive autonomy.
    
- **National Memory Culture Differences**
    Variations in how WWII is framed within each country’s broader historical narrative.
    
- **Institutional Scale and Spectacle Norms**
    Differences in tolerance for immersive or technologically prominent installations.
    
- **Educational Mandate Emphasis**
    Comparative expectations around public pedagogy versus experiential engagement.
    
- **Narrative Framing and Political Sensitivity**
    Differences in public tolerance for controversial historical interpretations.

Students then categorize each contradiction as:

- Productive (drives innovation)
- Destabilizing (threatens legitimacy)
- Containable (manageable through policy)
- Structural (embedded in funding or governance architecture)

## Part III — Change Laboratory Simulation

Change Laboratory is not used here as abstract theory. It functions as a structured rehearsal space for institutional intervention under constraint. Students simulate the internal deliberations that would occur if museum leadership, curators, technologists, donors, and educators evaluated the proposal. Using adapted Change Laboratory phases, students engage in:

1. Mirroring — Surface tensions revealed by intervention
2. Analysis — Map systemic roots of contradiction
3. Modeling — Propose reconfigured alignment
4. Examination — Stress-test against economic logics
5. Reflection — Evaluate governance stability

This is simulated but grounded in real institutional research.

---
## Economic Logic Overlay

Students must identify which tensions relate to:

- Production logic (infrastructure ownership)
- Exchange logic (funding and donor dynamics)
- Consumption logic (visitor experience)
- Distribution logic (labor and access fairness)

This reveals multi-layered institutional pressures. These logics frequently conflict. For example:

- Production logic may favor infrastructure permanence.
- Exchange logic may favor donor visibility.
- Consumption logic may favor immersive intensity.
- Distribution logic may favor equitable labor representation.

Students must identify which logic dominates each institutional configuration and how that dominance shapes implementation feasibility.

---
## Governance & Authority of Trust

Institutional viability depends not only on technical feasibility but on authority of trust. Each team must articulate:

- How representational authority is preserved under technological mediation
- How transparency in funding and implementation is communicated
- How historical gravity is protected from spectacle inflation
- How public trust would be reinforced rather than destabilized

Governance is treated as a structural condition of legitimacy, not an administrative afterthought.

---
## **AI and the Second Stimulus**

The introduction of AI into institutional systems introduces additional ambiguity in authorship, labor, and governance. Students must address:

**Are AI agents framing tools (instrumental WHY/WHAT) or actors emerging within Division of Labour (active WHO within distributed institutional labor)?**

Teams must map how each interpretation restructures the activity system and where authority migrates as a result. 

Important clarification:

**The second stimulus in MC6 is the previsualization system.**

AI may augment that system. But AI does not replace it.

If AI is integrated into previsualization, students must ask:

- Does AI reshape how contradictions are seen?
- Does it alter what becomes visible?
- Does it bias modeling outcomes?
- Does it privilege spectacle over restraint?

This is epistemological, not technological.
