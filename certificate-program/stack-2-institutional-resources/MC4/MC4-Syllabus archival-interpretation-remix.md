## Course Description

This microcredential marks the transition from **persona construction (Stack 1 — Processes)** to **archival mediation within institutional contexts (Stack 2 — Resources)**.

Students engage directly with archival material from the BBC television series *The World at War* (1973), treating the archive not as passive historical record, but as a **mediating resource within institutional systems**. Engagement with WWII archival materials requires historical seriousness. 
Assignments do not treat archival footage as aesthetic raw material detached from context. Students must demonstrate citation, contextual accuracy, and narrative discipline.

The core shift:

- **Stack 1 (Processes):** You created voice.
- **Stack 2 (Resources):** You work with voice that already exists.

The focus is not WWII history as content. The focus is how historical media is constructed, narrated, institutionalized, and remixed.

MC4 includes two distinct but related components:

1. **Case Study 2A (Comparative Analysis)**
    
    Cameron Crowe vs. Adam Curtis — institutional narrative construction.
    
2. **WWII Audio Production Assignment (Soundtrap)**
    
    Archival narrative construction using _The World at War_.Students produce two audio artifacts using Soundtrap: 
    - A collaborative narrative podcast (review or historically grounded screenplay format)
    - An ambient archival remix composition built exclusively from spoken-word excerpts

This assignment is explicitly:

- Not ironic remixing  
- Not meme culture  
- Not aesthetic experimentation detached from historical gravity  

This is disciplined archival engagement.

## Historical Frame: 1973 as Media Turning Point

MC4 operates within a layered temporal structure:

- **1930s–1945** → The historical period  
- **1973** → The BBC constructs that period for television  
- **Today** → You reconstruct the reconstruction  

This layered temporality matters.

1973 sits between:

- Postwar modernism  
- The rise of broadcast authority  
- John Berger’s *Ways of Seeing* (1972)  
- The emergence of archival montage as institutional persuasion  

The BBC’s *The World at War* is not raw history.  
It is already interpretation.

Your task is to understand and work inside that mediated layer.

## Core Theoretical Frame (Explicit)

This microcredential introduces the **5Ws+H as a coordinated set of lenses**.

Not merely narrative tools, but system diagnostic tools.

- **WHO** — Authorial voice / Subject position  
- **WHAT** — Mediating artifacts (archival footage, editing techniques, broadcast form)  
- **WHERE** — Institutional context (BBC, Rolling Stone, Hollywood, media industries)  
- **WHEN** — Historical layering (WWII vs. 1973 vs. today)  
- **HOW** — Narrative construction and montage  
- **WHY** — Framing logic / Value proposition / Ideological orientation  

Students will use these lenses to compare:

- Adam Curtis (BBC institutional montage filmmaker)  
- Cameron Crowe (Rolling Stone journalist turned Hollywood filmmaker)

## Case Study A (Stack 2A): Filmmaker vs. Journalist

### Adam Curtis vs. Cameron Crowe

This mirrors Stack 1’s Hayes vs. Hunter S. Thompson persona case study, but shifts focus from individual voice and platform construction to institutional embedding and archival authority. Students move from constructing persona (Process focus) to working within resource-governed systems (Resource focus).

The emphasis now shifts from persona → institution.

Students analyze:

| Dimension | Adam Curtis | Cameron Crowe |
|------------|-------------|----------------|
| Institutional Base | BBC | Rolling Stone → Hollywood |
| Primary Medium | Archival montage documentary | Narrative film / music journalism |
| Voice Position | Omniscient narrator | Embedded observer |
| Community of Practice | Public broadcasting | Rock journalism / film production |
| Resource Access | BBC archives | Music scenes + studio systems |
| Authority Mode | Institutional montage | Scene credibility |

Students analyze how Crowe’s authorship evolved within commercial music journalism and studio filmmaking structures, while Curtis operates inside a publicly funded archival institution with different governance, editorial mandates, and narrative obligations. The purpose is to expose:

- How media makers operate inside institutional ecosystems  
- How archives shape storytelling authority  
- How communities of practice legitimize voice

## Weekly Structure

### Week 1: Archive as Resource

**Focus:** *The World at War* as constructed archive  

Activities:
- Watch Episode 1 (opening bookend)
- Watch Episode 26 (closing bookend)
- Identify narrator authority tone
- Document voice, pacing, rhetorical structure

**Deliverable:**  
500-word archival voice analysis using 5Ws+H lenses  

---

### Week 2: Journalist vs. Filmmaker Case Study

Students analyze Curtis and Crowe across:

- Institutional constraints  
- Access to resources  
- Narrative construction style  
- Voice authority model  

**Deliverable:**  
Comparative matrix (2–3 pages)  
Explicit 5Ws+H mapping applied to both creators  

---

### Week 3: Soundtrap Production Planning

Teams of 2 form.

Choose one narrative path:

#### Option A: WWII Episode Review Podcast  
Collaborative critical review of Episodes 1 and 26.

#### Option B: Historically Grounded Fiction  
A scripted narrative rooted in actual WWII archival material.

**Constraints:**
- Must use spoken excerpts from *The World at War*
- All quoted material must be cited
- Tone must reflect historical seriousness

---

### Weeks 4–5: Production Phase

Two parallel audio artifacts required:

#### 1. Narrative Podcast (8–12 minutes)
- Review OR screenplay format
- Structured argument
- Institutional awareness visible in analysis

#### 2. Archival Ambient Composition (3–6 minutes)
- Non-lyrical
- Built from spoken archival excerpts
- No additional human voice allowed
- Soundtrap instrumentation permitted

This forces disciplined engagement with source material.

---

### Week 6: Critical Reflection

Written component (1000–1500 words):

Students address:

- How archival mediation differs from persona construction (Stack 1)
- How collaboration altered authorship
- Where institutional context shaped narrative decisions
- What changed when moving from individual voice to mediated voice
