
# # MICROCREDENTIAL 5: INSTITUTIONS, INSTALLATIONS & ARCHIVAL SYSTEMS

## Re-Visualization, Museum Mediation, and Resource Coordination

**Derived from**: Cultural Icons & Popular Art (2002–2006) + Institutional Installation Case Studies  
**Format**: Teams of 3–4  
**Primary Tools**: Pre-Visualization Platform (Institution-selected), Presentation Software (Slides/Keynote/Canva), Archival Research Tools  
**Estimated Duration**: 6–8 weeks  
**Credit Hours**: 3.0 CEUs  

---

## Course Description

Microcredential 5 shifts Stack 2 from **individual archival voice (MC4)** to **institutional resource coordination**. In other words, MC5 shifts from audio authority to spatial authority.

If MC4 asked:  
> How do individuals work with archival voice?

MC5 asks:  
> How do institutions coordinate archives, space, technology, and public authority?

Students move from Soundtrap-based audio production to institutional pre-visualization environments.

The assignment is not to design an event.

It is to:

1. Re-visualize WWII historical settings grounded in archival audio created in MC4.
2. Pre-visualize a museum-based digital installation for two institutional contexts:
   - The Canadian War Museum (Ottawa)
   - The National WWII Museum (New Orleans)
3. Extract archival knowledge into a structured trivia corpus suitable for future educational programming.

This microcredential introduces institutional mediation without yet introducing Change Laboratory (that belongs to MC6).

## Theoretical Frame (Explicit)

MC5 deepens three base-layer dimensions of Engeström’s activity system:

- **Rules of Engagement & Exchange (WHERE)**
- **Communities of Practice (WHAT)**
- **Division of Labour (WHO)**

### Eco, Hyperreality, and Institutional Responsibility

MC5 also introduces a critical representational tension drawn from Umberto Eco’s work on hyperreality.

When institutions deploy immersive technologies (LED facades, projection systems, large-scale digital mediation), there is a risk that representation becomes more vivid than historical evidence.

Hyperreality occurs when:

- The reconstruction exceeds the archive.
- Emotional intensity outruns documentation.
- Spectacle begins to substitute for historical grounding.

Students must therefore evaluate:

- Where visualization enhances understanding.
- Where visualization risks aesthetic overreach.
- How institutional authority regulates representational intensity.

This is not an anti-technology stance.

It is a discipline of institutional responsibility.

### Clarifications:

- **WHAT = Communities of Practice**
  Museums, historians, archivists, educators, curators
- **WHERE = Rules of Engagement & Exchange**
  Institutional policy, licensing, exhibition standards, funding constraints.
- **WHO = Division of Labour**
  Curators, designers, technologists, educators, donors, administrators.

Students must recognize:

The installation is not just a screen in a space.  
It is a coordination of institutional resources under constraints.

## Case Study 2B: ClearLED & The National Arts Centre (Ottawa)

Students analyze:

- ClearLED’s transparent media façade installation
- Institutional architecture integration
- Authorization processes
- Technical infrastructure requirements
- Branding + cultural positioning

This case study demonstrates:

Installations are tool ecologies, not screens. Students must evaluate how architectural media technologies balance institutional authority with the risk of hyperreal spectacle.

Deliverable: Institutional process mapping slide deck (analysis only — no design yet).

# Learning Outcomes

Upon completion, students will be able to:

1. Translate archival audio into historically grounded visual-spatial design.
2. Distinguish imaginative re-visualization from “Disneyfication.”
3. Analyze institutional constraints shaping installation design.
4. Coordinate archival content with architectural affordances.
5. Produce structured educational knowledge artifacts.
6. Compare and contrast institutional contexts across national frameworks.

# Weekly Structure

---

## Week 1: Institutional Context Analysis

Teams assigned (3–4 students).

Research both institutions:

- Mission statements
- Architectural design
- Exhibition philosophy
- Visitor demographics
- Educational programming
- Technological infrastructure (if available publicly)

Deliverable:
Institutional Comparative Brief (5–7 pages)

---

## Week 2: Re-Visualization (Historical Grounding)

Students return to their MC4 audio work.

Task:

- Identify key WWII locations referenced.
- Reconstruct spatial environments grounded in historical documentation.
- Avoid fictional embellishment.

Constraint:

Imagination must be tempered by historical evidence. Students must balance immersive visualization with archival fidelity, avoiding spectacle that distorts historical seriousness. Students must explicitly identify:

- What elements are documented.
- What elements are interpretive.
- What elements risk hyperreal amplification.

Design decisions must be annotated to distinguish archive from enhancement.

Deliverable:
Historical Re-Visualization Package
- Mood boards
- Spatial diagrams
- Source citations

---

## Week 3–4: Installation Pre-Visualization

Students must design a digital installation using provided institutional specs (real or simulated):

For BOTH:

- Canadian War Museum
- National WWII Museum (U.S.)

Components required:

1. Screen placement diagrams
2. Visitor flow modeling
3. Audio integration strategy
4. Lighting/environmental considerations
5. Accessibility compliance
6. Interpretive text design
7. Hyperreality Risk Assessment Slide
   - Where spectacle risk appears
   - How institutional guardrails are applied
   - What historical constraints limit visual intensity

Technology may include:
- Transparent LED (ClearLED case reference)
- Standard projection
- Immersive multi-screen configuration

Deliverable:
Dual Installation Pre-Visualization Deck (15–20 slides)

---

## Week 5: Educational Translation

From *The World at War*, each team extracts:

- 10 historically accurate trivia questions
- Full citation for each
- Difficulty tier labeling
- Rationale for educational value

Students contribute curated trivia questions (with citations) as additions to a shared institutional knowledge resource, intended for future educational activation. These questions are structured for hypothetical competitive high school programming (modeled after Reach for the Top). 

Important:

This is knowledge extraction as resource building.

Deliverable:
Trivia Corpus (10 questions + citations)
(contributions to a shared knowledge resource)

Students are not designing the trivia competition event itself.

They will:

- Pre-visualize how a finals-stage configuration would be situated within the existing architectural constraints of each museum.
- Model screen placement, audience sightlines, and spatial integration.
- Use Reach for the Top as an initial design reference for competition format.

They will not design:
- Game rules
- Event choreography
- Production logistics
- Hosting structure

---

## Week 6: Integration & Institutional Reflection

Written Team Reflection (1500–2000 words):

Students examine how national memory cultures shape curatorial norms, technological integration, and audience engagement expectations. For example:

- How institutional rules shaped design decisions.
- Differences between Canadian and American museum contexts.
- Where historical gravity limited creative latitude.
- How re-visualization differs from MC2 imaginative pre-visualization.
- What tensions emerged between archive, technology, and public education.
- Where did your design approach risk hyperreality, and how did your team regulate it?
