# MC2 Assignment: Hotel Ballroom Cultural Transformation

## Assignment Overview

**Title**: Applying ARIES Methodology to Design Cultural Event Experience  
**Type**: Team Project (3-4 students per team)  
**Estimated Time**: 40-50 hours per team (distributed across Weeks 4-7)  
**Format**: Visual pre-visualization + team documentation + individual reflection  
**Weight**: 60% team deliverable + 40% individual components = 100% of MC2 grade

---

## Purpose & Learning Objectives

This assignment applies the ARIES (Autopoietic Remix for Intertextual Ecological Systems) methodology to transform a generic hotel/conference ballroom into an immersive cultural experience. Students learn professional event design through three coordinated layers: Cultural Reservoir (what cultural inputs), Experiential Assembly (how they coordinate spatially/temporally), and Value Objectification (why it's economically viable).

### Learning Objectives

Students will be able to:

1. **Analyze venue transformation challenges** (blank canvas ballroom → immersive cultural experience)
2. **Apply ARIES three-layer methodology** (Reservoir → Assembly → Objectification)
3. **Research licensing/permissions** (IP rights, music, venue permits)
4. **Create client-facing pre-visualizations** (professional-quality pitch materials)
5. **Work collaboratively in remote teams** (distributed design coordination)
6. **Understand hospitality industry constraints** (budgets, operations, feasibility)

## Context: Learning from The American (Advanced Exemplar)

### Weeks 1-3: Analyzing Casablanca @ The American

**What you studied:**

In the first three weeks, you analyzed how experienced designers tackled a **complex challenge**:
- **The American bar** has existing brand identity (mid-century Americana, craft cocktails, Main Street location)
- **Casablanca film** brings Moroccan/French colonial cultural atmosphere
- **Tension:** How do you coordinate bar's American identity WITH film's Moroccan themes?

**What you learned:**

**ARIES Layer 1 (Cultural Reservoir):**
- Film iconography (Rick's Café, romantic mythology, WWII nostalgia)
- Moroccan cultural elements (cuisine, music, visual aesthetics)
- Venue identity (American bar positioning, existing operations)
- Bourdain legacy connection (Birthday Week programming)

**ARIES Layer 2 (Experiential Assembly):**
- Spatial transformation (bar becomes café through lighting, décor, temporary elements)
- Temporal sequencing (arrival → cocktails → screening → dinner → reflection)
- Atmospheric coordination (sound, scent, visual, taste layering)
- Service choreography (staff movements, timing, flow)

**ARIES Layer 3 (Value Objectification):**
- Licensing complexity (*Casablanca* rights, music rights, venue permits)
- Budget modeling (fixed costs, variable costs, revenue projections)
- Feasibility analysis (break-even, margins, risks)
- Cultural capital value (preserving Bourdain legacy = non-monetary benefit)

**Key insight from exemplar:**

The American designers had to **negotiate with existing venue identity**. They found unexpected connections (Rick's Café = American expat bar, The American = American-themed bar) and used temporary overlays (lighting, music, décor) to transform without permanent changes.

**This is sophisticated, professional-level work.**

**Task 2: Propose Cultural Theme**

Choose a cultural icon/theme to bring INTO the ballroom. This should be **external culture** that transforms the space.

**What makes a good theme?**

**Strong themes coordinate across multiple cultural dimensions:**
- **Film + Cuisine + Music** (like Casablanca @ The American)
- **Historical Event + Food + Performance** (e.g., 1920s Harlem Renaissance)
- **Author/Literary + Culinary + Atmospheric** (e.g., James Baldwin + soul food)
- **Music Genre + Cultural Context + Food** (e.g., Fado + Portuguese cuisine)

**Weak themes are too narrow:**
- Just a movie screening (not enough to transform entire space)
- Just a meal (ballroom becomes restaurant, not cultural immersion)
- Just music performance (concert, not coordinated experience)

**Examples of strong themes:**

**Film-Based:**
- *Tampopo* (1985 Japanese film) + Ramen night + Itamae performance
- *Babette's Feast* (1987) + French cuisine + Classical music
- *Eat Drink Man Woman* (1994) + Taiwanese banquet + Family storytelling

**Music-Based:**
- New Orleans Jazz Funeral + Gumbo/Po'boys + Second line parade
- Fado Night + Portuguese cuisine + Live fado performance + Port wine
- Tango Salon + Argentine asado + Live tango orchestra

**Author/Literary:**
- James Baldwin Tribute + Soul food + Harlem Renaissance jazz + Readings
- Gabriel García Márquez + Colombian cuisine + Magical realism performance
- Ocean Vuong + Vietnamese family meal + Poetry readings

**Historical/Cultural:**
- Harlem Renaissance (1920s) + Soul food + Jazz + Speakeasy atmosphere
- Paris 1920s (Lost Generation) + French café + Expatriate writers + Absinthe
- Berlin Cabaret (1920s-30s) + German cuisine + Weimar-era music

**Bourdain-Connected (if applicable):**
- Bourdain's Tokyo episode + Izakaya atmosphere + Yakitori + Sake
- Bourdain's Lyon episode + Bouchon cuisine + French wines
- Bourdain's Vietnam episode + Street food + Family-style dining

**What to avoid:**

❌ **Theme too local:** "Vancouver craft beer night" in Vancouver ballroom = redundant (ballroom is already in Vancouver)
✓ **Better:** "Belgian beer café" in Vancouver = brings external culture

❌ **Theme too generic:** "Italian dinner" = vague, not specific cultural immersion
✓ **Better:** "Sicilian family feast" = specific region, cultural context, family-style service

❌ **Theme requires specialized venue:** "Sushi omakase" needs chef's counter, not ballroom
✓ **Better:** "Izakaya night" works in ballroom (communal tables, shared plates)

**Your proposal should include:**

1. **Theme title** (evocative, clear)
2. **Cultural coordination concept** (how do film/music/food/atmosphere layer?)
3. **Why this ballroom?** (what affordances does this space offer?)
4. **Initial feasibility check** (is licensing possible? Cuisine achievable? Budget reasonable?)
5. **Visual inspiration** (3-5 reference images showing mood/atmosphere you're aiming for)

**Deliverable format:** Google Slides presentation (5-7 slides)
- Slide 1: Title (Theme + Ballroom)
- Slide 2-3: Ballroom documentation (specs, photos, constraints)
- Slide 4-5: Cultural theme proposal (concept, coordination, feasibility)
- Slide 6-7: Visual inspiration (mood boards, reference images)

### Week 5: ARIES Layer 1 + 2 Development

**Deliverable**: Draft Pre-Visualization (Miro board or equivalent, 15-20 pages/slides)

**Task 1: Cultural Reservoir (ARIES Layer 1)**

Document ALL cultural inputs for your theme. This is research-intensive.

**Required categories:**

**1. Primary Cultural Icon:**
- If film: Key scenes, characters, dialogue, visual aesthetics
- If music: Genre history, key artists, instrumentation, performance traditions
- If author/literary: Key works, themes, biographical context, cultural impact
- If historical event: Timeline, key figures, cultural significance, visual references

**2. Culinary Program:**
- What cuisine/dishes align with theme?
- Traditional recipes vs. adapted versions (for ballroom kitchen capabilities)
- Serving style (plated? Family-style? Buffet? Food stations?)
- Beverages (signature cocktails, wines, beers, non-alcoholic options)
- Dietary accommodations

**3. Musical/Audio Program:**
- What music accompanies experience?
- Live performance vs. recorded playlist?
- How does music coordinate with other elements (background? Feature?)
- Specific artists, tracks, genres

**4. Visual/Atmospheric Elements:**
- Lighting design (color, intensity, movement)
- Décor (centerpieces, table settings, wall treatments)
- Textiles (tablecloths, napkins, draping, banners)
- Signage, wayfinding, informational materials
- Scent (if applicable—incense, flowers, food aromas)

**5. Performance/Interaction:**
- Any live performers? (musicians, actors, dancers, speakers)
- Guest participation? (interactive elements, rituals, activities)
- Storytelling moments? (welcome speech, cultural context, narration)

**6. Intertextual References:**
- What other cultural texts does your theme connect to?
- How does theme circulate in broader culture?
- Why is this theme recognizable/resonant to guests?

**Format:** Visual board with images, quotes, references, notes
- Think: Pinterest board meets research documentation
- Every element should have visual reference + brief description
- Include sources/citations for research

**Task 2: Experiential Assembly (ARIES Layer 2)**

Design HOW all cultural elements coordinate in space and time.

**2A: Spatial Design**

**Floor Plan:**
- Ballroom layout (tables, stage, bar, food stations, entrance/exit)
- Guest flow (arrival → coat check → reception → seating → restroom access)
- Sightlines (can all guests see stage/screen? Any obstructed views?)
- Service flow (how do staff move? Kitchen to tables? Bar to guests?)

**Zoning:**
- Entry experience (first impression as guests arrive)
- Main dining area (table configuration, centerpieces)
- Performance space (stage, screen, musicians)
- Bar/beverage area (cocktails, wine service)
- Support areas (coat check, restrooms, kitchen staging)

**Atmospheric Transformation:**
- How do you make generic ballroom feel like [your theme]?
- Lighting (color washes, spotlights, projections, uplighting)
- Textiles (draping walls/ceiling to hide generic features)
- Centerpieces (what's on each table?)
- Entrance treatment (how do guests transition from hotel hallway into your world?)

**Visual mockups:**
- Floor plan diagram (annotated)
- Table setting photo/rendering (what does one table look like?)
- Entry experience rendering (first impression view)
- Stage/performance area rendering

---

**2B: Temporal Design**

**Event Timeline (minute-by-minute):**

Example structure (adjust for your theme):

**6:00 PM - Doors open**
- Guests arrive, check coats, receive welcome materials
- Reception area: Cocktails, light appetizers
- Background music (curated playlist, sets atmosphere)

**6:30 PM - Transition to seating**
- Gentle announcement, guests find tables
- Lights dim slightly, music transitions

**6:45 PM - Welcome / Context Setting**
- Host introduces theme (5-7 minutes)
- Cultural context (why this theme? What will you experience?)
- Acknowledge venue support, sponsors (if applicable)

**7:00 PM - First Course + Performance/Film begins**
- Service begins (appetizer or salad)
- Performance starts (live music? Film screening?)
- Staff choreography (service without disruption)

**7:45 PM - Main Course**
- Primary dish served
- Performance/film continues or transitions
- Timing coordination (food hot when it arrives, not sitting under heat lamps)

**8:30 PM - Dessert + Reflection**
- Sweet course
- Performance concludes or reaches contemplative moment
- Guests can linger, discuss, absorb

**9:00 PM - Event ends**
- Guests depart at leisure (not rushed)
- Music transitions to departure atmosphere
- Coat retrieval, goodbyes

**Critical timing considerations:**

- How long is film/performance? (Coordinate with meal timing)
- When do staff serve? (Between scenes? During quieter moments?)
- How do you handle late arrivals? (Don't disrupt others)
- What if event runs long? (Venue rental time limits)
- Bathroom breaks? (Intermission? Or design for graceful exits/returns?)

**2C: Atmospheric Orchestration**

How do sensory elements layer throughout event?

**Lighting Journey:**
- Arrival: Warm, welcoming (guests feel comfortable)
- Seating: Transitional dimming (signal event beginning)
- Performance/Film: Dark, focused (attention directed)
- Service: Subtle uplighting (staff can see to serve without disrupting)
- Departure: Gradual brightening (signal graceful conclusion)

**Sound Journey:**
- Arrival: Ambient cultural music (sets mood, not too loud for conversation)
- Seated: Music transitions (volume adjusts as performance begins)
- Performance: Feature audio (film dialogue, live music, spoken word)
- Transitions: Music bridges (between courses, between performance segments)
- Departure: Return to ambient (guests can chat, reflect)

**Scent Journey (if applicable):**
- Arrival: Subtle (incense? Flowers? Fresh bread?)
- Service: Food aromas (intentional, not accidental)
- Avoid: Overpowering scents (some guests sensitive), competing smells

**Taste Journey:**
- Progression: Light → Rich → Sweet (classic meal structure)
- Cultural authenticity vs. accessibility (balance familiar/unfamiliar)
- Beverage pairings (wine? Beer? Cocktails? How do they coordinate?)

**Visual Journey:**
- Entry: Immediate transformation (leave hotel behind)
- Tables: Detailed cultural references (centerpieces, menus, settings)
- Performance: Dynamic visuals (film projection? Stage lighting? Performers?)
- Departure: Lingering impressions (what do guests remember seeing?)

### Week 6: ARIES Layer 3 + Refinement

**Deliverable**: Complete Pre-Visualization with Budget/Feasibility Analysis

**Task: Value Objectification (ARIES Layer 3)**

Prove your event is economically viable, socially feasible, culturally desirable.

**3A: Authorization Matrix**

What permissions/licenses do you need?

**Intellectual Property:**
- Film screening rights (if applicable)
  - Public performance license required
  - Cost estimate (contact distributor? Research typical rates?)
  - Example: *Casablanca* is public domain (free), but most films aren't
- Music licensing
  - SOCAN (Canada) or ASCAP/BMI (US) fees
  - Live performance vs. recorded music (different rights)
  - If using specific songs: mechanical licenses
- Literary rights (if using readings from books/poems)

**Venue Permits:**
- Liquor license (does ballroom have one? Need special event permit?)
- Food service permits (health department, if bringing external caterer)
- Fire safety (capacity limits, exit requirements)
- Noise ordinances (local bylaws about music volume, end times)

**Insurance:**
- Event liability insurance (required by venue?)
- Liquor liability (if serving alcohol)
- Performer insurance (if hiring musicians, actors)

**Format:** Spreadsheet or table

| Permission Needed | Source/Authority | Estimated Cost | Timeline to Secure | Notes |
|-------------------|------------------|----------------|---------------------|-------|
| Film screening rights | Warner Bros | $500-1,000 | 4-6 weeks | Need venue details for quote |
| SOCAN music license | SOCAN Canada | ~$200 | 2 weeks | Based on attendance |
| Liquor permit | City licensing | $0 (venue has) | N/A | Included in venue rental |
| ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |

**3B: Budget Model**

What does this event cost? What revenue does it generate?

**Fixed Costs (regardless of attendance):**
- Venue rental (ballroom fee)
- AV equipment rental (if not included)
- Licensing fees (film, music, permits)
- Décor (centerpieces, textiles, signage)
- Entertainment (performer fees, if applicable)
- Marketing/promotion (invitations, social media ads, posters)
- Staffing base (event coordinator, setup crew)

**Variable Costs (scale with attendance):**
- Catering (food cost per person)
- Beverages (bar consumption)
- Staffing service (servers, bartenders, based on guest count)
- Printed materials (menus, programs, place cards per guest)
- Party favors or takeaways (if applicable)

**Revenue Streams:**
- Ticket sales (primary revenue)
- Bar sales (if not included in ticket)
- Sponsorships (if applicable—local businesses, cultural organizations)
- Merchandise (if applicable—related books, music, etc.)

**Sample Budget Structure:**

**Scenario**: 120 guests, $75 ticket price

**Revenue:**
- Ticket sales: 120 × $75 = $9,000
- Bar revenue: 120 × $15 avg = $1,800
- Sponsorship: $1,000 (local business)
- **Total Revenue: $11,800**

**Fixed Costs:**
- Venue rental: $2,000
- AV rental: $500
- Licensing (film, music): $800
- Décor/props: $1,200
- Performers: $1,500
- Marketing: $300
- **Total Fixed: $6,300**

**Variable Costs (per person):**
- Food: $35/person × 120 = $4,200
- Beverage (wine/beer included): $10/person × 120 = $1,200
- Service staff: $8/person × 120 = $960
- Printed materials: $2/person × 120 = $240
- **Total Variable: $6,600**

**Total Costs: $12,900** (Fixed $6,300 + Variable $6,600)

**Profit/Loss: -$1,100** (Revenue $11,800 - Costs $12,900)

**Analysis:** This event LOSES money at $75/ticket. Would need to:
- Increase ticket price to $85-90 (break-even to modest profit)
- OR reduce costs (cheaper venue? Simpler décor? Fewer staff?)
- OR increase attendance (economies of scale—fixed costs spread across more guests)

**Create three scenarios:**
1. **Conservative**: Low attendance, higher costs = worst case
2. **Moderate**: Expected attendance, realistic costs = likely outcome
3. **Optimistic**: High attendance, some cost savings = best case

**Show break-even analysis:**
- At what ticket price does event break even?
- At what attendance level (if ticket price fixed)?
- What's minimum viable event? (Smallest scale that's still worthwhile?)

---

**3C: Feasibility Assessment**

Is this event actually doable?

**Economic Feasibility:**
- ✓ Can we price tickets at level that covers costs?
- ✓ Is there market demand at that price point?
- ⚠ What's risk if attendance is lower than projected?

**Operational Feasibility:**
- ✓ Can ballroom kitchen produce this menu?
- ✓ Can we secure licensing in time?
- ✓ Do we have access to performers/equipment needed?
- ⚠ What's backup plan if key element falls through?

**Cultural Feasibility:**
- ✓ Is there audience for this theme in this city?
- ✓ Does theme translate across cultures? (If local audience is diverse)
- ⚠ Are we appropriating culture inappropriately? (Respectful vs. exploitative?)

**Timeline Feasibility:**
- ✓ How much lead time needed? (Venue booking, licensing, promotion)
- ✓ What's critical path? (What must happen first before other things can proceed?)
- ⚠ What's realistic timeline from concept to execution? (3 months? 6 months?)

**SWOT Analysis:**

**Strengths:**
- What advantages does this event have?
- Strong theme coordination? Unique offering? Great venue?

**Weaknesses:**
- What vulnerabilities?
- High costs? Niche audience? Logistical complexity?

**Opportunities:**
- What could make this even better?
- Partnership with cultural organization? Media coverage? Repeat series?

**Threats:**
- What could go wrong?
- Licensing denial? Performer cancellation? Low ticket sales?

**Honest Assessment:**

Would you ACTUALLY recommend this event to a client/venue?
- If YES: Why? What makes it viable?
- If NO: What would need to change to make it viable?
- If MAYBE: What further research/validation needed?

This is professional work—be honest about feasibility, not just optimistic.

## Common Student Questions

**Q: "Can we design for a venue that's not a hotel ballroom?"**  
A: You must use hotel/conference ballroom for this assignment. Why? (1) Standardized constraints everyone shares, (2) hospitality industry relevance, (3) removes venue negotiation complexity. Advanced work (after MC2) can tackle unique venues.

**Q: "What if our ballroom has some unique features (chandeliers, windows, architectural details)?"**  
A: That's fine! "Generic" means no competing cultural identity, not that space must be featureless. Use architectural features as design opportunities.

**Q: "Can we propose an event series instead of single event?"**  
A: Focus on designing ONE event thoroughly. In feasibility section, you can note "this could be monthly series" but don't design all iterations—that's too much scope.

**Q: "What if our budget shows event loses money?"**  
A: BE HONEST. Show the loss, then explain: (1) how to fix it (higher price? Lower costs?), OR (2) why it's worth doing anyway (cultural value, building audience, loss leader for venue). Don't fake numbers to force profitability.

**Q: "Do we need to actually contact venue for real pricing?"**  
A: If possible, yes (cold email or call event sales). If not accessible, research online (many hotels publish event packages) or estimate based on comparable venues. Document your sources.

**Q: "Can we use AI tools for mood boards, renderings, budget templates?"**  
A: Check with instructor. Typically: AI for inspiration/drafting = OK. AI as primary creator without human refinement = not OK. Always disclose AI use.

**Q: "What if teammates don't contribute equally?"**  
A: Document your contributions clearly in Individual Statement. Peer evaluations are confidential—assess teammates honestly. Instructor adjusts individual grades based on contribution evidence.

**Q: "Can we change our theme after Week 4 proposal?"**  
A: Small adjustments OK (refine concept). Major pivots (completely new theme) = ask instructor. Ideally commit to theme by Week 4 so you have time to develop it thoroughly.

## Tips for Success

### Research Deeply, Design Thoughtfully

**Don't:**
- Pick theme because "it sounds cool" without researching cultural context
- Design event you'd personally attend without considering broader audience
- Ignore budget realities ("we'll figure out funding later")
- Create beautiful visuals without operational feasibility

**Do:**
- Research cultural significance, historical context, current relevance
- Design for audience beyond your own taste (who else would this serve?)
- Face budget constraints honestly, design within realistic means
- Balance aesthetics with operations (beautiful AND doable)

### Coordinate Across ARIES Layers

**Warning signs of poor coordination:**
- Layer 1 (Reservoir) includes elements that never appear in Layer 2 (Assembly)
- Layer 2 (Assembly) proposes things not budgeted in Layer 3 (Objectification)
- Layer 3 (Objectification) budget doesn't match Layer 2 design scope

**Good coordination:**
- Every cultural input (Layer 1) appears spatially/temporally (Layer 2)
- Every design element (Layer 2) has cost estimate (Layer 3)
- Budget (Layer 3) enables design vision (Layer 2) which manifests cultural theme (Layer 1)

**Test:** Can you trace any element through all three layers?
- "Moroccan lanterns" (Layer 1 cultural input) → Centerpiece on each table (Layer 2 spatial) → $40 each × 15 tables = $600 (Layer 3 budget)
- If element appears in Layer 1 but not 2 or 3 = coordination failure

### Embrace the Blank Canvas

**Students often worry:** "Generic ballroom is boring. How do we make it special?"

**Reframe:** Blank canvas = pure transformation challenge.
- You're not negotiating with existing identity (like The American designers had to)
- You're building world from scratch (like theater set design)
- Every choice is intentional (nothing is "already there")

**Opportunities:**
- No competing aesthetics (ballroom accepts any transformation)
- No operational conflicts (no existing menu/bar/style to work around)
- Total creative control (within budget/logistics constraints)

**The challenge:**
- Must create atmosphere entirely through your design (no venue identity to lean on)
- Guests arrive from generic hotel hallway—how do you transport them immediately?

### Respect Cultural Sources

**Cultural appropriation concerns are real.**

**Problematic approaches:**
- Surface-level stereotypes (sombreros for "Mexican" theme, geishas for "Japanese")
- Ignoring painful history (plantations, colonialism, oppression made "aesthetic")
- Treating culture as costume (wear/eat/perform without understanding significance)

**Respectful approaches:**
- Deep research (read books, watch films, consult cultural experts if possible)
- Honor complexity (cultures aren't monolithic, avoid stereotypes)
- Acknowledge sources (credit artists, chefs, musicians, cultural traditions)
- Partner when possible (work with cultural organizations, community members)

**Ask:**
- Are we representing culture authentically or stereotypically?
- Would members of this culture feel honored or exploited?
- Are we learning from culture or extracting from it?
- Who benefits? (Guests' experience? Cultural visibility? Mutual respect?)
