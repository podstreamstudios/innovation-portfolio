## MICROCREDENTIAL 3: VALUE PROPOSITIONS & PLATFORM ECOSYSTEMS

### Using The Artifact as Applied Case Study

**Derived from**: Cultural Icons & Popular Art (IAT 203) + ARIES Methodology  
**Format**: 4-5 weeks, structured cohort (remote teams)  
**Platform**: Strategyzer online tools + Miro (canvas work)  
**Meeting Schedule**: Weekly synchronous sessions + asynchronous team work  
**Credit Hours**: 2.0 CEUs (Continuing Education Units)

---

## Course Description

This microcredential uses Strategyzer's Business Model Canvas and Value Proposition Canvas to analyze The Artifact—a vinyl record documenting the transformation of songs from a Vancouver bar through digital curation, AI processing, and physical re-materialization.

Students map the **eight platforms** involved (Shazam, Apple Music/Spotify, karaoke-version.com, lalal.ai, Soundtrap, SoundCloud, elasticStage/Super Specific Vinyl, pressing plants), identifying:

- **Value propositions** for each stakeholder
- **Revenue streams** and cost structures
- **Customer segments** and relationships
- **Key resources, activities, and partnerships**
- **Contradictions** between platforms (Engeström's activity theory tensions)
- **Value capture vs. value creation** disparities

The course culminates in students designing **alternative platform configurations** that better balance creator needs, rights holder compensation, platform sustainability, and cultural access.

---

## Learning Outcomes

Upon successful completion, students will be able to:

1. **Apply Strategyzer tools** (Business Model Canvas, Value Proposition Canvas) to analyze platform ecosystems
2. **Map multi-sided platforms** showing how different stakeholders extract/create value
3. **Identify contradictions** between activity systems using Engeström's framework
4. **Evaluate value capture disparities** (who benefits, who bears risk, who's excluded)
5. **Design alternative business models** that address identified tensions
6. **Assess legal/ethical risks** within platform value propositions
7. **Connect business model analysis to cultural production realities**

## The Artifact's Eight Platforms: BMC Analysis Framework

### Students create Business Model Canvases for each:

---

### **1. SHAZAM (Apple)**

**Value Propositions**:

- For users: Instant song identification, discovery
- For Apple: User data, ecosystem lock-in, Apple Music funnel

**Customer Segments**:

- Music listeners (curious about unknown songs)
- Apple ecosystem users
- Artists/labels (discovery platform)

**Key Resources**:

- Acoustic fingerprinting database
- AI/ML pattern matching algorithms
- Apple infrastructure

**Key Activities**:

- Song identification
- Database maintenance
- User data collection

**Revenue Streams**:

- Apple Music subscriptions (conversion funnel)
- User data monetization
- FREE to users (subsidized by Apple ecosystem)

**Cost Structure**:

- Database licensing/maintenance
- AI processing infrastructure
- Marketing

**Contradictions**:

- **Free service** (no direct revenue) but **valuable data** (indirect monetization)
- **Discovery tool** (helps all platforms) but **Apple ecosystem funnel** (competitive advantage)

---

### **2. APPLE MUSIC (vs. Spotify comparative)**

**Value Propositions**:

- For users: Streaming catalog, curated playlists, Shazam integration
- For artists: Streaming revenue, discovery
- For Apple: Subscription revenue, ecosystem retention

**Customer Segments**:

- Music listeners (subscription model)
- Apple device owners (vertical integration advantage)
- Artists/labels (distribution platform)

**Key Partnerships**:

- Shazam (owned by Apple - vertical integration)
- Record labels (licensing agreements)

**Revenue Streams**:

- Subscription fees (~$10.99/month)
- **Higher artist payouts** than Spotify (competitive positioning)

**Contradictions**:

- **Vertical integration** (Shazam → Apple Music seamless) vs. **Platform lock-in** (anti-competitive?)
- **Artist-friendly positioning** (higher payouts) vs. **Still extractive** (streaming economics)

---

### **3. KARAOKE-VERSION.COM**

**Value Propositions**:

- For creators: Legal instrumental versions without complex licensing
- For rights holders: Mechanical royalties from cover market
- Platform: Intermediary revenue

**Customer Segments**:

- Karaoke performers
- Content creators (YouTube, TikTok)
- DJs/remixers
- Educational users

**Key Resources**:

- Licensing agreements with publishers
- Professional studio musicians (or AI generation)
- Instrumental catalog

**Key Activities**:

- License negotiation
- Instrumental production/sourcing
- Platform maintenance

**Revenue Streams**:

- Per-track sales (~$2.99)
- Subscription tiers (volume licensing)

**Cost Structure**:

- Licensing fees to publishers
- Studio production (or AI processing)
- Platform operations

**Contradictions**:

- **Legal intermediary** (enables legitimate use) but **Market gaps** (not all songs available)
- **Professional quality** vs. **Cover versions** (not always matching original arrangements)
- **Price point** (~$3/track) creates incentive for **unlicensed alternatives** (lalal.ai)

---

### **4. LALAL.AI**

**Value Propositions**:

- For users: Affordable AI stem separation ($1.50 vs. $400 pro software)
- Democratizes audio processing (no technical skill required)

**Customer Segments**:

- Remix artists
- DJs
- Content creators
- Karaoke users
- Educators (?)
- **Rights infringers** (unintended but real customer segment)

**Key Resources**:

- AI/ML models (trained on...?)
- Cloud processing infrastructure
- User-friendly interface

**Key Activities**:

- AI model training/refinement
- Audio processing
- Platform maintenance

**Revenue Streams**:

- Pay-per-use (~$1.50/song)
- Subscription tiers
- API access for developers

**Cost Structure**:

- AI processing (compute costs)
- Model training data (licensing? scraping?)
- R&D

**Contradictions**:

- **TOS requires users certify rights** (liability shield) but **Service enables infringement** (practical use case)
- **Democratizes tools** (good for creators) vs. **Enables piracy** (bad for rights holders)
- **Revenue depends on gray area use** but **Must maintain legal cover**
- **Training data** (where from? licensed? fair use? scraped?)

---

### **5. SOUNDTRAP FOR EDUCATION**

**Value Propositions**:

- For students: Browser-based DAW (no software installation)
- For educators: Affordable music production platform
- For Spotify (owner): Educational market capture, future customer funnel

**Customer Segments**:

- Educational institutions (bulk licensing)
- Students (free/subsidized access)
- Educators (teaching tools)

**Key Partnerships**:

- Educational institutions (licensing agreements)
- Spotify (corporate owner)

**Revenue Streams**:

- Institutional licenses
- Freemium subscriptions for individuals
- **Subsidized by Spotify** (ecosystem play, not profit center)

**Cost Structure**:

- Cloud infrastructure
- Development/maintenance
- Educational discounts (margin compression)

**Contradictions**:

- **Educational tool** (assumes legitimate content) but **No verification** of source material rights
- **Spotify ownership** (music company) but **Enables derivative works** (potential infringement)
- **Freemium model** (accessible) vs. **Professional features limited** (drives paid upgrades)

---

### **6. SOUNDCLOUD**

**Value Propositions**:

- For artists: Free hosting, discovery, community
- For listeners: Underground/emerging artists, DJ mixes, remixes
- Platform: Ad revenue, subscriptions, partnerships (elasticStage)

**Customer Segments**:

- Independent/underground artists
- DJs/remixers
- Music listeners (discovery-focused)
- **Gray area content creators** (remixes, bootlegs, mashups)

**Key Activities**:

- Content hosting
- Content moderation (with gaps)
- Community building
- Monetization partnerships

**Revenue Streams**:

- Ads (free tier)
- Subscriptions (SoundCloud Go+)
- Artist tools (Repost, distribution)
- **elasticStage partnership revenue**

**Contradictions**:

- **Content ID enforcement** (removes individual infringing tracks) vs. **Tolerates continuous mixes** (same content, different format)
- **Requires rights certification** (elasticStage vinyl) vs. **Hosts gray area content** (DJ culture)
- **Platform depends on underground culture** vs. **Trying to legitimize/monetize**
- **Vinyl pressing enablement** (elasticStage) while **Content still potentially infringing**

---

### **7. ELASTICSTAGE (SoundCloud Partnership)**

**Value Propositions**:

- For artists: On-demand vinyl with no upfront cost
- For fans: Purchase physical vinyl directly
- For SoundCloud: Revenue share, artist retention, platform differentiation
- For elasticStage: Manufacturing revenue, SoundCloud user base access

**Customer Segments**:

- SoundCloud artists (especially indie/small-batch)
- Fans/collectors (vinyl enthusiasts)

**Key Partnerships**:

- SoundCloud (platform integration)
- Pressing plants (manufacturing)
- Shipping/fulfillment (logistics)

**Revenue Streams**:

- Vinyl sales ($38.90/unit)
- Revenue split: ~$11.67 to artist, remainder to elasticStage + SoundCloud

**Cost Structure**:

- Pressing/manufacturing
- Shipping/fulfillment
- Platform integration
- Customer service

**Contradictions**:

- **Requires rights certification** (artist liability) but **No verification** (platform deniability)
- **SoundCloud uploads may be infringing** but **Platform enables vinyl pressing** (monetizing infringement?)
- **Artist takes all legal risk** but **Platform/elasticStage extract most value** ($38.90 sale, artist gets $11.67)
- **On-demand model** (no inventory) but **Higher per-unit cost** (no economies of scale)

---

### **8. SUPER SPECIFIC VINYL**

**Value Propositions**:

- For customers: Custom vinyl for "occasions" (gifts, milestones, novelty)
- Personal/emotional value vs. commercial vinyl culture
- Low barrier to entry (single unit orders)

**Customer Segments**:

- Gift-givers (birthdays, weddings, anniversaries)
- Niche collectors
- Personal memory preservation
- **Educational/demonstration projects** (like The Artifact)

**Key Resources**:

- Kickstarter funding (community-backed)
- Pressing partnerships
- Novelty/occasion marketing angle

**Revenue Streams**:

- Per-unit sales ($39-49)
- Kickstarter backer rewards

**Cost Structure**:

- Pressing/manufacturing (on-demand)
- Kickstarter fulfillment
- Marketing ("occasion" positioning)

**Contradictions**:

- **"Occasion" framing** (personal, non-commercial) but **Same copyright issues** (doesn't matter legally if it's a gift)
- **Kickstarter funding** (community trust) vs. **Unproven service** (fulfillment risk)
- **Novelty positioning** (not "serious" vinyl) but **Same manufacturing process** as traditional pressing

## Week 2 Activity: Ecosystem Value Flow Mapping

### Students create visual diagram showing:

**WHO CREATES VALUE**:

- Original artists (creative labor)
- Cascade Room (curation, atmosphere)
- You (curation, arrangement, performance)
- AI platforms (processing, separation)
- Pressing plants (manufacturing)

**WHO CAPTURES VALUE** (monetarily):

- Karaoke-version.com: $2.99/track
- lalal.ai: $1.50/track
- Soundtrap: $0 (Spotify subsidy)
- SoundCloud: Revenue share on elasticStage sales
- elasticStage: ~$27/unit (of $38.90)
- Super Specific: $39-49/unit
- Traditional plant: ~$25/unit

**WHO BEARS RISK**:

- **You**: Legal liability for rights certification (all platforms)
- **Platforms**: Contributory infringement risk (if sued)
- **Original artists**: No compensation for YouTube-sourced tracks, no consent

**WHO'S EXCLUDED**:

- **Original artists/rights holders**: Not consulted, not compensated (for unlicensed tracks)
- **Listeners at Cascade Room**: Unaware their soundscape became commercial artifact
- **Future creators**: Licensing costs prohibitive, driven to unlicensed alternatives

### Visual representation:

```
VALUE CREATION           VALUE CAPTURE          RISK
─────────────           ──────────────         ────
Artists (songs)    →    karaoke: $3/track      You (legal liability)
Cascade Room      →    lalal.ai: $1.50/track   Platforms (contrib. inf.)
You (curation)    →    elasticStage: $27/unit  
AI (processing)   →    SoundCloud: revenue%    
Plant (manufacture) →  You: $11.67/unit OR     
                       full cost depending     
                       on model                

Value flows UP (to platforms)
Risk flows DOWN (to individual creator)
Original artists often EXCLUDED entirely
```

## Week 3: Contradiction Matrix

### Students map contradictions using Engeström's framework:

**PRIMARY CONTRADICTIONS** (within single element):

|Platform|Internal Contradiction|
|---|---|
|**SoundCloud**|Free hosting (value prop) vs. Content moderation costs (expense)|
|**lalal.ai**|Democratizing tools (mission) vs. Enabling infringement (effect)|
|**elasticStage**|Artist empowerment (marketing) vs. Artist exploitation (revenue split)|

**SECONDARY CONTRADICTIONS** (between elements of same activity):

|Platform|Subject ↔ Instrument|Rules ↔ Object|
|---|---|---|
|**SoundCloud**|Artist needs hosting ↔ Platform needs ad revenue|TOS prohibits infringement ↔ DJ mixes tolerated|
|**karaoke-version**|Creator needs cheap license ↔ Platform needs revenue|Licensing law ↔ Market gaps|

**TERTIARY CONTRADICTIONS** (current vs. emerging practice):

|Current System|Emerging System|Tension|
|---|---|---|
|Licensed karaoke tracks|AI-generated instrumentals|Legal clarity vs. affordability|
|Traditional pressing (bulk)|On-demand vinyl|Economies of scale vs. accessibility|
|Platform enforcement (Content ID)|Creator circumvention (continuous mixes)|Arms race|

**QUATERNARY CONTRADICTIONS** (between neighboring activities):

|Activity System 1|Activity System 2|Contradiction|
|---|---|---|
|Streaming culture (rent, infinite access)|Vinyl culture (own, scarcity)|Different value props|
|Educational use (fair dealing)|Commercial distribution (licensing required)|Same artifact, different legal status|
|Platform revenue (monetize content)|Artist revenue (compensate for content)|Value capture disparity|

## Week 4: Alternative Platform Design Challenge

### Teams choose ONE of these redesign prompts:

---

### **Option A: Redesign karaoke-version.com**

**Problem identified**:

- Market gaps (not all songs available)
- Price point ($3/track) creates incentive for unlicensed alternatives
- No revenue to original artists (only publishers get mechanical royalties)

**Redesign challenge**: Create alternative business model that:

1. Fills market gaps (how to handle unavailable tracks?)
2. Reduces price barrier (can it be cheaper while still compensating rights holders?)
3. **Shares revenue with original artists** (not just publishers)
4. Remains economically sustainable

**Tools**: Value Proposition Canvas + Business Model Canvas

**Evaluation criteria**:

- Legal feasibility
- Economic sustainability
- Artist compensation fairness
- Creator accessibility

---

### **Option B: Redesign SoundCloud + elasticStage**

**Problem identified**:

- Platform contradictions (enforce copyright vs. enable infringement monetization)
- Value capture disparity (artist gets $11.67 of $38.90)
- Rights certification without verification (liability on artist)

**Redesign challenge**: Create alternative platform integration that:

1. Resolves copyright enforcement contradiction
2. Better revenue split for artists
3. Verification mechanism for rights certification (or transparent gray area acknowledgment)
4. Sustains platform economically

**Could explore**:

- Transparent sourcing tiers (fully licensed, fair use claimed, unlicensed-for-education)
- Artist compensation even for fair use claims
- Platform insurance for rights disputes
- Peer review for educational fair use claims

---

### **Option C: Design "EduPress" - Educational Vinyl Platform**

**Problem identified**:

- No legitimate path for educational cultural documentation with mixed licensing
- Existing platforms force false certifications or prohibitive costs
- Educational fair use unclear for physical artifact production

**Design challenge**: Create platform specifically for educational cultural documentation projects:

1. Transparent sourcing tier system (licensed / owned+AI / fair use claimed)
2. Peer review process for fair use claims
3. Limited distribution (institutional only, max copies)
4. **Optional artist compensation** even when claiming fair use
5. Open documentation (all projects public, provenance visible)

**Questions to address**:

- Revenue model (how does platform sustain itself?)
- Legal defensibility (does peer review provide cover?)
- Artist participation (would rights holders engage?)
- Scalability (can this work beyond tiny niche?)

---

### **Option D: Design "FairShare" - Direct Artist Compensation Platform**

**Problem identified**:

- Creators use unlicensed sources (YouTube + AI) because licenses unavailable or expensive
- Artists receive no compensation for these uses
- Guilt/ethical discomfort but no practical alternative

**Design challenge**: Platform that enables:

1. Creators admit what they couldn't license
2. Direct compensation to artists (voluntary or required?)
3. Sliding scale based on creator's budget/revenue
4. **Retroactive licensing** (pay artists after project succeeds)

**Model possibilities**:

- Patreon-like micro-patronage
- Kickstarter for retroactive licensing
- "Honor system" voluntary payment
- Escrow account (funds released when identified)
